ACTION RESEARCH

 

  Action Research Definitions

Action research examines phenomena in a natural setting with the researcher  in the role of the subjective facilitator. 

Action research is a type of research where the researcher must intervene to assist with learning and awareness about the world as a social construct, to make sense of, and affect the social construct of the organization

Is where the researcher must investigate behavior patterns that must be altered to deal with change. (Barnett and Mackness, 1983)

Action research simultaneously assists in practical problem solving and expand scientific knowledge, as well as enhances the competences of the respective actors, being performed collaboratively in an immediate situation using data feedback in a cyclical process aimed at an increased understanding of a given social situation, primarily applicable for the understanding of change processes in social systems and undertaken within a mutually acceptable framework. (Hult and Lennung’s, 1980)

An Investigation of a phenomenon of interest in one or more organisation where the researcher is involved (usually in a consultant role with quite deliberate intervention) and the impact of the researcher must be acknowledge and identified. (Nunamaker et. al., 1991)

  Characteristics of Action Research

  • Provides a framework for a holistic type of intervention into complex and messy situations where systems are viewed as social systems

  • Provides a basis for good communication and facilitation issuing information for desirable and feasible change

  • Espouses a balance of theory-action-application-learning

  • Promotes creative and visionary thinking

  • Promotes shared understanding of other people’s perspectives

  • Provides focus for effective attention to issues within the organisational culture
